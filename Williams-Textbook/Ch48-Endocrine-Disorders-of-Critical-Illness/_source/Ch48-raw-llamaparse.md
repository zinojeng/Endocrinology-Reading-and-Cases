# KEY POINTS

- In critical illness, characteristic endocrine alterations develop; these alterations differ in the acute versus prolonged phase of illness and complicate the diagnosis of preexisting endocrine disease.
- Acute critical illness is characterized by low levels of thyroid hormone and insulin-like growth factor 1, and high levels of cortisol, which is presumably adaptive and mainly brought about by peripheral alterations in hormone metabolism.
- In patients with prolonged critical illness, a central suppression of the somatotropic, thyrotropic, and adrenocorticotropic axes progressively develops, which may impair rehabilitation, although treatment indications and regimens remain to be studied in adequately powered trials.
- Safe blood glucose control in critically ill patients requires a validated protocol, with accurate and frequent blood glucose measurements and avoidance of insulin boluses.
- The ideal blood glucose target in critically ill patients not receiving early parenteral nutrition remains unclear.
- Critically ill patients have low vitamin D concentrations, which is probably adaptive in the acute phase of illness.

# Introduction

As a consequence of severe medical illness, major surgery, or trauma, critically ill patients need vital organ monitoring or support to prevent or treat life-threatening insults, in order to maintain or restore homeostasis. As part of the stress response, profound endocrine alterations occur, including neuroendocrine changes, insulin resistance and stress hyperglycemia, and alterations in vitamin D, which have been assumed to be adaptive. However, in patients who remain dependent on intensive care for a prolonged period of time, a different endocrine phenotype develops, which may be maladaptive. The illness-induced endocrine alterations often complicate the diagnosis of preexisting endocrine disease and may have important therapeutic implications. This chapter provides a review of the most prominent endocrine changes during acute and prolonged critical illness, as well as the diagnostic and potential clinical implications.

# Alterations in the Somatotropic Axis

In the acute phase of critical illness, growth hormone (GH) secretion increases, with increased pulse frequency and amplitude and elevated trough concentrations. Similarly, peripheral growth hormone resistance occurs, with lowered expression of hepatic GH receptors, leading to low concentrations of insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF1), IGF-binding protein 3 (IGFBP3), and acid-labile subunit (ALS). Low levels of binding proteins also lead to increased elimination of IGF1, enhancing low IGF1 bioavailability. High GH concentrations directly induce lipolysis and gluconeogenesis, hence increasing substrate to vital tissues for survival, while indirect and energy-consuming anabolic effects of GH—mediated by IGF1—are postponed. Systemic inflammation is likely a main driver of these changes, although the concomitant fasting response may also play a role.

In patients developing prolonged critical illness, IGF1 concentrations remain low, predominantly explained by a lack of hypothalamic stimulation, since GH pulse amplitude and trough concentrations decline while peripheral GH responsiveness at least partially recovers. Administration of the ghrelin-like GH-releasing peptide 2 (GHRP2), and even more the combination of GHRP2 and GH-releasing hormone (GHRH), restore the pulsatility of GH secretion with a proportional increase in IGF1, its binding proteins IGFBP3, and ALS. In acute critical illness, levels of the active form of thyroid hormone, triiodothyronine (T₃), rapidly decline, explained by altered peripheral metabolism (Fig. 48.2).¹⁵ Mechanistically, the activity of type 1 deiodinase is attenuated while type 3 deiodinase activity increases, leading to increased peripheral conversion of thyroxine (T₄) secreted by the thyroid gland to the biologically inactive reverse T₃ (rT₃). Simultaneously, degradation of T₃ is increased, altogether leading to low T₃ levels and a decreased T₃:rT₃ ratio.¹⁵ Concomitantly, concentrations of the pituitary hormone thyrotropin (thyroid-stimulating hormone [TSH]) and of T₄ briefly rise, followed by rapid normalization of TSH and T₄.¹⁶ These alterations have been designated as euthyroid sick syndrome, nonthyroidal illness, or acute low T syndrome. In prolonged critical illness, alterations in peripheral metabolism often persist: T₃ concentrations further decline due to the virtually absent pulsatile TSH secretion, and this results in low TSH and T₄ concentrations.¹⁵ The low pituitary hormone secretion is explained by hyposecretion of thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH). An autopsy study revealed low TRH gene expression in patients dying after prolonged critical illness compared with sudden death.¹⁹ Moreover, TRH administration can augment nonpulsatile TSH release in prolonged critically ill patients, and TRH coadministered with GHRP2 also reactivates pulsatile TSH secretion.³,4 The drivers of central suppression of the thyroid axis in prolonged critical illness remain unclear. Endogenous dopamine and hypercortisolemia may play a role.⁶,20,21 In addition, an altered setpoint for feedback inhibition may be present through increased local type 2 deiodinase activity.22,23 Apart from the critical illness–induced alterations, TSH secretion and/or thyroid hormone production may be suppressed by iatrogenic factors, including iodine-containing wound dressings and contrast agents, and drugs such as glucocorticoids, somatostatin, dopamine, and amiodarone.²⁴

The common alterations in thyroid hormone metabolism in critical illness have important diagnostic implications because the diagnosis of true thyroid dysfunction and preexisting thyroid disorders can be extremely difficult.²⁵ In critically ill patients who do have overt primary hypothyroidism, the TSH concentrations may be lower than expected. Hence, a low or normal TSH does not exclude primary hypothyroidism.²⁵ To facilitate diagnosis, history, physical examination, and the presence of autoantibodies may provide diagnostic clues. However, repeat thyroid tests after recovery from critical illness are usually necessary to confirm the diagnosis.²⁵ Conversely, elevated T₃ concentrations are highly unusual in critical illness and should always lead to suspicion of hyperthyroidism or even thyroid storm.²⁵

From a clinical point of view, the question is whether alterations in thyroid hormone secretion and metabolism should be treated or not. In the acute phase of critical illness, these alterations are assumed to be adaptive.² Interventional studies supplementing thyroid hormone in the acute phase of critical illness have not shown benefit on outcomes.26–30 Moreover, several metabolic interventions that improve the outcomes of critically ill patients—tight glucose control, as well as withholding early parenteral nutrition supplementing insufficient enteral nutrition—further lower T₃ and the T₃:rT₃ ratio, which seems to be a beneficial adaptation, whereas a lowering of T₄ appears to be deleterious.31–33 In contrast to the adaptive response in critical illness of short duration, several observations suggest that prolonged low T₃ levels and low hypothalamic TRH secretion in prolonged critical illness may be harmful.⁴,34–36 Observational studies revealed potential signs of adaptation to prolonged low T₃ in some tissues, including increased expression of thyroid hormone transporters,³⁴ a relatively increased expression of the active thyroid hormone receptor TRa1 over the ligand-insensitive isoform TRa2,³⁶ and altered local deiodinase activity resulting in increased local thyroid hormone availability.³⁵ Moreover, pilot studies found suppressed markers of catabolism by administration of TRH in combination with GHRP2 to prolonged critically ill patients.⁴ However, RCTs that are powered for clinical endpoints are lacking. There is currently no solid evidence on how to adapt endocrine treatment for patients with preexisting thyroid disorders.

# Alterations in the Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal Axis

Critically ill patients undergo profound changes in the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Following acute stress, adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) briefly rises, accompanied by elevated cortisol concentrations (Fig. 48.3).³⁹ Thereafter, ACTH concentrations rapidly decline to low levels, while cortisol concentrations remain persistently elevated.⁴⁰ The persistent hypercortisolemia in the presence of suppressed ACTH (ACTH-cortisol dissociation) after the hyperacute phase is explained by suppressed breakdown of cortisol. Moreover, less cortisol binding to the binding proteins cortisol-binding globulin (CBG) and albumin due to lower levels and CBG binding affinity will increase the active free cortisol fraction.40–42 High cortisol availability during acute stress is adaptive, as illustrated by the development of adrenal crisis in patients with no or insufficient adrenal reserve to mount a cortisol increment to stress.⁴³ As part of the vital “fight or flight” response, hypercortisolemia promotes hemodynamic stability by provoking fluid retention and by sensitizing the response to catecholamines, and it aids in shifting the body from energy-consuming anabolism to a catabolic state with liberation of energy substrate to vital tissues.⁴⁴ Recent evidence indicates that the rapid suppression of ACTH following acute stress is explained by negative feedback inhibition through stress-induced hypercortisolemia on the pituitary gland, while ongoing stress-induced hypothalamic stimulation through corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) and arginine-vasopressin (AVP) leads to ongoing expression of the ACTH precursor pro-opiomelanocortin (POMC), which is no longer processed into ACTH.⁴⁵ Unprocessed POMC leaks into the circulation, leading to elevated circulating POMC concentrations.⁴⁵ The potential role of elevated POMC in critical illness remains largely unknown. POMC may stimulate adrenal cortisol production and secretion to some extent,⁴⁶ although in vivo evidence in critically ill models is currently lacking.

In prolonged critically ill patients, central suppression of the HPA axis develops while cortisol breakdown remains reduced.⁴¹ The central suppression of the HPA axis is illustrated by the progressive decline of total and free cortisol concentrations to even normal concentrations after 4 weeks in the ICU in the absence of elevated ACTH, despite persisting illness.⁴¹ Moreover, the ACTH response to a CRH stimulation test significantly decreases beyond the first week in the ICU,⁴⁷ whereas POMC remains elevated.⁴⁵ Adrenal glands from patients dying after prolonged critical illness are characterized by a reduced expression of ACTH-regulated genes and a severely disturbed adrenocortical structure, potentially explained by the reduced trophic effect by low ACTH.⁴⁸ One week after ICU discharge of long-stay ICU patients, both ACTH and cortisol concentrations rise to supranormal concentrations, which corroborates a central and reversible suppression of the HPA axis by a yet unknown factor.⁴¹ Five years after critical illness, alterations in the HPA axis normalize in survivors.¹⁴

Apart from alterations in cortisol production and degradation in acute and prolonged critical illness, recent evidence also reveals tissue-specific alterations in cortisol effects through altered local cortisol production by 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 1 (11β-HSD1) and through altered expression of the glucocorticoid receptor and its co-chaperone FK506 binding protein 51 (FKBP51).⁴⁹ A study in samples from critically ill patients documented reduced glucocorticoid action in human neutrophils based on the reduced expression of glucocorticoid-induced leucine zipper (GILZ).⁴⁹ Such reduced glucocorticoid action in neutrophils is accompanied by reduced gene expression of the glucocorticoid receptor α (GRα), which mediates most glucocorticoid effects, and by increased expression of the GRα-sensitivity–regulating co-chaperone FKBP51, which negatively regulates GRα action.⁴⁹ Moreover, very high glucocorticoid availability, as defined by receiving high-dose synthetic steroids (≥30 mg methylprednisolone over 48 hours) or free cortisol concentrations ≥3.096 μg/dL (the 75th percentile of untreated patients), is not associated with increased glucocorticoid activity in human neutrophils.⁴⁹ In contrast, in monocytes, skeletal muscle and adipose tissue obtained from critically ill patients, and in vital tissues of septic mice, glucocorticoid action was mostly increased, with a tissue-specific regulation of local glucocorticoid availability through altered expression of 11β-HSD1, GRα, and its negative regulator FKBP51.⁴⁹ These tissue-specific alterations in expression of 11β-HSD1, GRα and FKBP51, and glucocorticoid action, as deducted from expression of GILZ, occurred in the acute phase of critical illness and largely persisted in the prolonged phase of critical illness.⁴⁹

# Insulin Resistance and Stress Hyperglycemia

In response to severe inflammation and elevations in counter-regulatory hormones, critically ill patients usually develop hyperglycemia, which has been associated with poor outcomes. However, the ideal glucose target remains unclear, because RCTs have yielded at first sight nonconcordant results. Whereas three single-center RCTs performed in Leuven, Belgium, have shown benefit from lowering blood glucose to the healthy, age-adjusted fasting range (50–80 mg/dL for infants, 70–100 mg/dL for children older than 1 year, and 80–110 mg/dL for adults) compared with tolerating hyperglycemia up to the renal threshold (215 mg/dL), the NICE-SUGAR RCT subsequently showed harm by tight glucose control compared with maintaining blood glucose below 180 mg/dL. The outcome differences between these landmark RCTs have been attributed to differences in glucose targets in the control group, differences in accuracy of the glucose monitoring and the insulin protocol, and differences in feeding strategies (Table 48.1). In this context, a benefit of tight glucose control was only demonstrated in a context of early parenteral nutrition supplementing insufficient enteral nutrition, which has been abandoned after RCTs showed harm by this feeding strategy, even when feeding-induced hyperglycemia is treated. Hence, it currently remains unclear whether tight glucose control is beneficial in the absence of early parenteral nutrition, when provided with a validated protocol that minimizes the risk of hypoglycemia and of large glucose fluctuations. This is currently being studied in the TGC-fast multicenter RCT (clinicaltrials.gov, NCT03665207).

Some evidence suggests that the glucose target may need to be individualized according to the chronic level of blood glucose control. Of note, observational studies have shown that the association of blood glucose with outcomes is affected by diabetic status and the pre-ICU level of glucose control, as derived from hemoglobin A1c concentrations (HbA1c). Patients with poorly controlled diabetes may be more vulnerable to severe and relative hypoglycemia, and observational studies have shown that the lowest mortality risk in these patients associates with blood glucose concentrations that are closer to their preadmission glucose levels. However, solid evidence supporting individualized glucose control is hitherto lacking. Whereas a before-after implementation study suggested benefit by implementing looser glucose control in diabetic patients, a large RCT did not demonstrate benefit of individualized, HbA1c-guided glucose control as compared to conventional control with a glucose target of less than 180 mg/dL. However, in this RCT, there was a large overlap in blood glucose between groups, in part because of delayed randomization, and the study is likely underpowered to detect or exclude a benefit of individualized blood glucose control.

Altogether, recent evidence questions the traditional concept of critical illness–related corticosteroid insufficiency (CIRCI), which has been assumed to be present in septic shock, and has been related to a decreased incremental cortisol response to ACTH, as well as to generalized glucocorticoid resistance. On the other hand, the central suppression of the HPA axis observed in long-stay ICU patients, and the rise of ACTH and cortisol 1 week after ICU discharge, suggest that central adrenal insufficiency may exist in a subset of long-stay ICU patients, which could clinically manifest as a persistent need for vasopressors, impaired consciousness, and electrolyte abnormalities. Future research should investigate the potential drivers of the central suppression of the HPA axis in critically ill patients.

These findings may have therapeutic implications. Indeed, stress doses of hydrocortisone to prevent adrenal crisis and as adjunctive treatment of septic shock far exceed the cortisol production rate in hyperinflammatory patients without adrenal insufficiency, which has been estimated to be approximately 60 mg per day. One could speculate that lower doses may suffice, potentially with fewer side effects, a hypothesis that needs to be investigated.

Although the optimal blood glucose target for critically ill patients remains unclear, recent evidence illustrates that safe and effective glucose control requires frequent and accurate glucose monitoring and a validated insulin protocol. Arterial blood glucose measurements are preferred, because venous measurements can be falsely elevated in cases of concurrent intravenous glucose infusion, and capillary measurements are potentially unreliable.

Insulin is preferably administered through continuous intravenous insulin infusion while avoiding boluses, to slowly progress toward blood glucose target and to avoid hypoglycemia and large glucose fluctuations, because both have been associated with poor outcomes. The quality of glucose control can be improved with use of (nearly) continuous glucose monitoring and computerized decision-support algorithms. However, the additional cost of (nearly) continuous glucose monitoring and the discussion about the ideal glucose target, as well as potential concerns regarding point accuracy of subcutaneous sensors due to inherent lag time compared with intravascular sensors, preclude widespread adoption of this technology.

The universal presence of stress hyperglycemia impedes the diagnosis of preexisting diabetes based on glucose determinations alone. Moreover, recent studies have shown that a considerable amount of critically ill patients may have previously unknown antecedent diabetes mellitus, as derived from elevated HbA1c levels on ICU admission. Therefore, routine HbA1c measurements may aid in discriminating pure stress hyperglycemia versus stress hyperglycemia superimposed on diabetes mellitus, which could be important for post-ICU follow-up. Traditionally, insulin resistance induced by stress has been considered to be fully reversible. However, recent studies suggest that the development of stress hyperglycemia may be a risk factor for developing subsequent diabetes mellitus. However, HbA1c was not systematically measured in these studies, which could be a confounding factor. Moreover, the definitions of hyperglycemia varied, and studies insufficiently corrected for known risk factors of diabetes, so it is not clear whether and to what extent stress diabetes independently associates with long-term diabetes. Future research should investigate this in greater detail and needs to elucidate which patients may need specialized endocrine follow-up.

# Conclusion

Critical illness is accompanied by neuroendocrine abnormalities, insulin resistance and stress hyperglycemia, and low vitamin D concentrations. These illness-induced changes are proportional to the severity of illness and are largely adaptive during acute severe illness, although the optimal blood glucose target remains to be determined.
