# Primary Aldosteronism: Diagnosis and Management Pearls National Taiwan University Hospital logo

* Suspect PA in resistant HTN, hypokalemia, adrenal incidentaloma, or early-onset HTN.
        - Normokalemia does not exclude PA
        - Do not dismiss PA just because the patient is already taking an MRA
        - *Screen for PA in all individuals with HTN (2025 ES CPG)*
* Screening is based on plasma aldosterone, renin, and aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR).
        - Preform screening in the morning with the patient seated, and avoid dietary sodium restriction for several days beforehand
        - Correct hypokalemia if possible before testing
        - Manage interfering medications depending on individual safety and feasibility
        - Suppressed renin is often a key clue.
* CT does not reliably determine laterality.
* Adrenal vein sampling (AVS) is often required before surgery in eligible patients.
* Unilateral disease is potentially curable; bilateral disease is usually treated with MRA.

# Adrenal Incidentaloma with MACS

* Every adrenal incidentaloma requires two parallel questions:
    * Is it benign or malignant?
    * Is it functioning or nonfunctioning?
* Initial endocrine workup should assess cortisol autonomy and consider PPGL and PA when indicated.
* 1-mg overnight DST is central to MACS evaluation.
* MACS is biochemical cortisol autonomy without overt Cushingoid features.
    * Lack of overt Cushingoid appearance does not exclude clinically meaningful cortisol excess.
* Clinical relevance may include hypertension, diabetes, obesity, osteoporosis, or subtler morbidity.
    * The key issue in MACS is not only the post-DST cortisol value, but also the patient’s clinical burden.
* Not every patient with MACS requires surgery, but selected patients may benefit.
* Surgery in MACS should be individualized rather than automatic.

# Cushing’s Syndrome

* Suspect CS in patients with unusual or progressive metabolic abnormalities.
* Important clues include
        * Young fragility fracture or secondary osteoporosis;
        * Proximal muscle weakness;
        * Facial plethora, easy bruising;
        * Purple striae.
* Exogenous glucocorticoid exposure must be excluded first.
* First-line screening tests include
        * 1-mg overnight DST
        * Late-night salivary cortisol
        * 24-hour urinary free cortisol
* After confirming endogenous hypercortisolism, ACTH is the key first branching test.
        * Low ACTH suggests ACTH-independent disease and points toward an adrenal source.

# Pheochromocytoma and Paraganglioma (PPGL)

* Suspect PPGL in patients with adrenal mass +/-headache, palpitations, diaphoresis, or labile hypertension.

* Initial biochemical tests are

    * <span style="color: blue">Plasma free metanephrines</span>

    * Urinary fractionated metanephrines

* Biochemical confirmation should precede invasive procedures.

* Never biopsy an adrenal mass before excluding PPGL.

* A negative family history does not exclude hereditary PPGL.

* <span style="color: blue">Germline testing</span> should be considered because it affects follow-up and family screening.

* All PPGLs have <span style="color: blue">metastatic potential</span>, lifelong surveillance is recommended

# Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)

National Taiwan University Hospital logo

* Consider CAH in patients with hyperandrogenism, or biochemical discordance of adrenal steroid profiles
* Most common type: 21-hydroxylase deficiency (genotype – phenotype correlation)
* Adult internal medicine practice most often encounters:
        - transition care
        - fertility issues
        - long-term glucocorticoid overtreatment
        - metabolic complications
* Asymptomatic nonpregnant adults with non-classic CAH often do not require routine glucocorticoid therapy.
* Pregnancy planning and specialist follow-up remain important.
